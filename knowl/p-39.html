The textbook points out that we often want to look specifically for factors of the form \(q(x)=x-c\text{,}\) which I will call an elementary linear factor. What happens if we attempt to divide a polynomial \(p(x)\) by \(x-c\text{?}\) If there is no remainder, then we have found a simple factor,

\begin{equation*} p(x) = (x-c) \cdot m(x) \end{equation*}

where \(m(x)\) is a polynomial with a degree smaller by 1 than the degree of \(p(x)\text{.}\) It will be like we have just peeled off one factor of the polynomial.
